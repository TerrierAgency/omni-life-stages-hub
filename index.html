Nutrition for Every Stage of Life

From their first wobbly steps to their golden years, your dog's nutritional needs change dramatically as they grow. Understanding what your dog needs at each life stage is the foundation of lifelong health — and the key to helping them thrive, not just survive.

Stage 1

Puppy Stage: 0 – 12 Months

The most rapid period of growth in your dog's life. Getting nutrition right now sets the foundation for everything that follows.

What puppies need nutritionally

Puppies grow at an extraordinary rate — some breeds increase their birth weight by 40-50 times in the first year alone. This rapid development requires a nutrient-dense diet that supports:

  • Skeletal growth — Calcium and phosphorus in the correct ratio (1.2:1) for strong, properly formed bones
  • Brain development — DHA (an Omega-3 fatty acid) is critical for cognitive function, learning, and trainability
  • Immune system building — Antioxidants, vitamins E and C, and zinc support the developing immune system during the vulnerable vaccination period
  • Muscle development — High-quality protein (at least 22% of diet) to build lean muscle mass
  • Digestive maturation — Prebiotics and easily digestible ingredients help establish healthy gut flora

Common puppy health concerns

  • Sensitive tummies — Puppies' digestive systems are still developing and can be easily upset by sudden diet changes, stress, or new environments
  • Skin developing — The skin barrier is not fully mature, making puppies more susceptible to dryness, irritation, and environmental allergens
  • Teething pain — Between 3 and 6 months, puppies lose their baby teeth. This can cause discomfort, reduced appetite, and chewing on everything in sight
  • Growth-related joint stress — Large and giant breeds are particularly prone to developmental orthopaedic conditions if nutrition is not properly balanced

Key Puppy Milestones

  • 3 – 6 months: Teething period — soft food or soaked kibble can help with discomfort
  • 6 – 16 weeks: Primary vaccination period — immune support is especially important
  • 6 months: Switch from 3-4 meals per day to 2 meals per day
  • 10 – 12 months: Begin gradual transition to adult food (larger breeds may wait until 14-18 months)

Feeding tips for puppies

  1. Feed a complete, age-appropriate puppy food — not adult food with added extras
  2. Divide daily portions into 3-4 meals until 6 months old, then reduce to 2 meals
  3. Always transition foods gradually over 7-10 days to avoid tummy upsets
  4. Avoid over-supplementing calcium, especially in large breeds — excess can cause skeletal abnormalities
  5. Weigh your puppy regularly and adjust portions based on growth charts from your vet

Stage 2

Adult Stage: 1 – 7 Years

Your dog's prime years. The focus shifts from growth to maintaining optimal health, managing weight, and preventing early decline.

Maintaining peak health

Adult dogs need a well-balanced diet that provides sustained energy without excess calories. The three pillars of adult dog nutrition are:

  • Weight management — Over 50% of UK dogs are overweight. Maintaining a healthy weight reduces the risk of diabetes, heart disease, and joint problems
  • Skin and coat health — A shiny, healthy coat is a visible indicator of good nutrition. Omega-3 and Omega-6 fatty acids are essential for skin barrier function
  • Digestive balance — A healthy gut microbiome supports immunity, nutrient absorption, and overall wellbeing

Common adult dog health issues

  • Skin irritation — Allergies and environmental sensitivities often first appear between ages 1-3. Itching, redness, and hot spots are common complaints
  • Early joint wear — Even in young adults, repetitive exercise and genetic predisposition can start to affect joint cartilage, especially in active and large breeds
  • Digestive sensitivity — Food intolerances, stress-related gut issues, and inflammatory bowel conditions can develop during adulthood
  • Dental disease — By age 3, most dogs show some signs of dental disease, which can affect eating habits and overall health

Activity levels and nutritional adjustments

Not all adult dogs need the same amount of food. Active working dogs, sporting breeds, and highly energetic dogs may need 20-40% more calories than sedentary or less active breeds. Conversely, neutered dogs and those with lower activity levels often need fewer calories to maintain a healthy weight.

Adjust portions based on body condition rather than breed guidelines alone. You should be able to feel your dog's ribs without pressing hard, and they should have a visible waist when viewed from above.

Signs to watch for

  • Persistent scratching, licking, or chewing at skin
  • Unexplained weight gain or loss
  • Dull, dry coat or excessive shedding
  • Stiffness after exercise or reluctance to play
  • Chronic loose stools or digestive irregularity
  • Bad breath or difficulty eating hard food

Stage 3

Senior Stage: 7+ Years

As your dog ages, their body changes. Targeted nutrition and supplementation can help them stay comfortable, mobile, and mentally sharp.

Changing metabolism

Senior dogs typically experience a 20-30% reduction in metabolic rate. This means they need fewer calories but often require higher concentrations of specific nutrients to maintain health. Without dietary adjustments, senior dogs are prone to weight gain, which accelerates age-related decline.

Common senior dog conditions

  • Arthritis and joint stiffness — The most common condition in senior dogs. Cartilage wears down over time, leading to pain, reduced mobility, and reluctance to exercise
  • Reduced immunity — The immune system weakens with age, making senior dogs more susceptible to infections, slower wound healing, and increased inflammation
  • Coat thinning and skin changes — Reduced oil production leads to a drier, less lustrous coat. Skin becomes thinner and more prone to injury
  • Cognitive decline — Canine Cognitive Dysfunction (similar to dementia in humans) affects up to 68% of dogs over 15. Signs include disorientation, altered sleep patterns, and reduced responsiveness
  • Weight change — Some seniors gain weight due to reduced activity, while others lose muscle mass (sarcopenia) despite adequate calorie intake

How to adapt diet and supplementation

Senior dogs benefit from a diet that is lower in calories but higher in quality protein to preserve lean muscle mass. Key nutritional adjustments include:

  • Increase Omega-3 fatty acids for anti-inflammatory support
  • Add glucosamine and MSM for joint cartilage maintenance
  • Include antioxidants (vitamins E and C) to combat oxidative stress
  • Consider medium-chain triglycerides (MCTs) for cognitive support
  • Ensure adequate fibre for digestive regularity
  • Feed smaller, more frequent meals to ease digestion

When to talk to your vet

  • Your dog is struggling to get up or showing obvious pain when moving
  • Significant appetite changes — eating much more or much less than usual
  • Unexplained weight loss, especially loss of muscle mass
  • Increased thirst and urination (can indicate kidney or endocrine issues)
  • Confusion, disorientation, or changes in sleep patterns
  • New lumps, bumps, or changes in existing ones
  • Persistent coughing, laboured breathing, or exercise intolerance

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about feeding and supplementing your dog at every life stage.

Most dogs should transition from puppy to adult food between 10 and 12 months of age. Larger breeds may benefit from staying on puppy food slightly longer — up to 14-18 months — as they take longer to reach full skeletal maturity. Always transition gradually over 7-10 days by mixing increasing amounts of adult food with the puppy food to avoid digestive upset.

Signs your senior dog may benefit from supplements include stiffness when getting up, reluctance to climb stairs or jump, a duller coat, increased tiredness, weight changes, or slower recovery from exercise. If your dog is over 7 years old and showing any of these signs, a vet-formulated supplement targeting joint health, immunity, or cognitive function can make a meaningful difference. Always consult your vet before starting a new supplement regimen.

It is generally not recommended to give puppies supplements formulated for adult dogs. Puppies have different nutritional requirements, and adult dosages may not be appropriate for their developing bodies. Some ingredients that are beneficial for adults could be excessive for puppies. Always choose age-appropriate supplements and consult your vet if you are unsure about what your puppy needs.

Puppies (0-12 months) generally need 3-4 meals per day until 6 months, then 2 meals per day, with higher calorie density to support growth. Adult dogs (1-7 years) do well on 2 meals per day, with portions based on weight, breed, and activity level. Senior dogs (7+ years) often need 10-20% fewer calories due to reduced metabolism, but may benefit from smaller, more frequent meals to aid digestion. Always follow your food manufacturer's guidelines and adjust based on your dog's body condition.

Common signs of nutritional deficiency in dogs include a dull or dry coat, excessive shedding, flaky skin, lethargy, poor wound healing, digestive issues such as chronic diarrhoea, weak nails, and unexplained weight loss. In puppies, growth abnormalities and skeletal issues can also indicate deficiency. If you notice any of these signs, consult your vet for a dietary review and potential blood work.
